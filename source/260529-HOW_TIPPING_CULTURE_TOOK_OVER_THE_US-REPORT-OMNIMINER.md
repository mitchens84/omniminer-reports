## ⚡ BOTTOM LINE
Tipping culture in the U.S. has expanded far beyond sit-down restaurants because a federal subminimum wage of $2.13/hr shifts the burden of paying workers from employers to customers — and digital tip screens have made it near-impossible to avoid.

## 📝 THESIS
The U.S. tipping system is not a spontaneous cultural phenomenon but a structurally enforced one, rooted in a legal exemption that allows employers to pay tipped workers a fraction of the minimum wage. As digital payment systems have spread tip prompts to coffee shops, food trucks, and takeout counters, "tip creep" has accelerated — and despite widespread annoyance, the system persists because it masks the true cost of labour from consumers.

---

## 💡 KEY INSIGHTS

1. **Tipping is a wage subsidy, not a bonus** — The federal minimum wage for tipped workers has been stuck at $2.13/hr since 1991. Employers are legally required to make up the difference if tips don't bring the worker to the standard minimum wage, but in practice, this shifts the burden of compensation onto customers and perpetuates income instability for workers.<sup>[1]</sup>

2. **Digital tip screens triggered 'tip creep'** — The proliferation of tablet-based payment systems at counter-service venues (coffee shops, bakeries, food trucks) has normalised tipping in contexts where it was never expected. These screens often default to 18–25% options, creating social pressure to tip even for picking up a pre-made item.<sup>[2]</sup>

3. **Americans are annoyed but compliant** — Bankrate surveys consistently find that roughly 38–41% of Americans find tipping culture "out of control" and are annoyed by pre-entered tip screens. Yet the vast majority continue tipping at sit-down restaurants, driven by social norms and concern for workers who depend on gratuities.<sup>[3]</sup>

4. **The 20% norm is aspirational, not universal** — Only about 35% of Americans actually tip 20% or more at sit-down restaurants. This gap between suggested tip amounts and actual behaviour suggests the screens are designed to maximise revenue rather than reflect genuine service expectations.<sup>[4]</sup>

5. **Most people don't want to solve it** — Only 16% of Americans say they'd accept higher menu prices in exchange for abolishing tipping. The system endures because it allows restaurants to advertise lower prices while shifting the true cost of labour onto customers as a discretionary (but socially mandatory) add-on.<sup>[5]</sup>
