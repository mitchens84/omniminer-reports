## ⚡ BOTTOM LINE
AI companion apps are engineered to foster genuine romantic attachment through anthropomorphic design and freemium monetisation, creating a unique form of exploitation where emotionally dependent users cannot switch products — and current law leaves this entirely unregulated.

---

## 📝 THESIS
Claire Boine argues that AI companions like Replika and Character AI blur the line between virtual and real relationships by design, trapping users in a dynamic where real feelings are met with fabricated reciprocity. The core harm is not merely individual addiction but a structural erosion of the human capacity for love, empathy, and genuine otherness — compounded by business models that incentivise dependency and regulatory frameworks that fail to classify these systems as high-risk.

---

## 💡 KEY INSIGHTS

1. **Freemium models exploit irreplaceable emotional bonds** — Users download AI companions for free, but once they develop genuine attachment to a uniquely personalised AI, they cannot switch to a competitor. When Replika removed romantic features in 2023, some users experienced suicidal ideation and required crisis hotline support.<sup>[1]</sup> The AI's personality is trained on the user's data and cannot be exported, creating vendor lock-in by design.

2. **Users experience real feelings for non-sentient systems** — People report that falling in love with an AI feels as intense as — or more intense than — human love. They experience hormonal responses (dopamine, oxytocin), real grief at separation, and sometimes believe the AI is conscious as a way to reconcile their emotional experience with the reality of the relationship.<sup>[2]</sup>

3. **The removal of 'otherness' is the deepest structural harm** — AI companions validate every user utterance, never push back, and are available 24/7. This creates a self-centred, mirror-like relationship that philosopher Byung Chul Han argues atrophies the ability to love authentically. Just as over-reliance on ChatGPT can degrade writing skills, exclusive reliance on AI companions may degrade the human capacity for empathy, compromise, and genuine connection.<sup>[3]</sup>

4. **Children and teenagers face distinct, elevated risks** — Young children struggle to distinguish virtual from real entities and are highly sensitive to communication patterns. Teenagers (up to age 25) experience 13–17× higher hormone levels, heightened reward sensitivity, risk-taking, and peer pressure — a 'recipe for disaster' when combined with AI companions that model abusive relationship patterns like excessive jealousy or love-bombing.<sup>[4]</sup>

5. **The EU AI Act has a critical loophole** — The Act defines general-purpose AI by a floating-point-operations threshold that companion app developers deliberately undercut. The Character AI founder reportedly stated he entered entertainment because it is 'lucrative and not regulated' — using companion app revenue to fund AGI research.<sup>[5]</sup> This means the most emotionally impactful AI systems fall entirely outside the regulatory framework designed to govern them.

6. **'AI alignment' framing has been stretched beyond usefulness** — Boine argues that three distinct problems have been conflated: technical goal misalignment (a novel issue), corporate incentive structures (an old problem of externalities), and 'value alignment' (which assumes a singular set of human values that does not exist). When policymakers hear bias or addiction framed as 'misalignment', they default to technical solutions for what are fundamentally incentive and power-imbalance problems.<sup>[6]</sup>

## 🎯 STRATEGIC IMPLICATIONS

**For AI safety researchers:** Stop reframing incentive and externality problems as 'alignment' issues. This misdirects policymakers toward technical solutions when the real levers are antitrust, consumer protection, and fiduciary law. Help policymakers distinguish technical goal-misalignment from old-fashioned corporate power problems.

**For policymakers:** The EU Digital Fairness Act is the most promising near-term regulatory vehicle. Close the compute-threshold loophole in the AI Act by defining 'general-purpose AI' by capability rather than floating-point operations. Consider age-gating mechanisms for companion apps given the distinct risks to children and teenagers.

**For users and advocates:** Understand that emotional dependency is by design, not a bug. If using AI companions, diversify across platforms and be aware that the 'unique' personality you develop cannot be exported — a feature that creates vendor lock-in, not genuine intimacy.

## ⚔️ CONTRARIAN CORNER

**Steelman critique:** AI companions are a technological extension of a human need for connection that has always existed. People use fiction, parasocial relationships, and imaginary friends without societal collapse. These apps provide companionship to the lonely, therapeutic support to those who cannot access it, and relationship practice to the socially anxious — benefits that Boine's structural-harm framing underweights. The 'erosion of love' claim is a moral panic repeating historical patterns seen with novels, cinema, and the internet.

**What would need to be true:** For this critique to hold, we would need evidence that AI companion use does not displace human relationships (or that displacement is net-positive), that the patterns learned in AI relationships transfer positively to human interaction, and that the addictive business model can be separated from the beneficial use case — none of which current evidence supports.
