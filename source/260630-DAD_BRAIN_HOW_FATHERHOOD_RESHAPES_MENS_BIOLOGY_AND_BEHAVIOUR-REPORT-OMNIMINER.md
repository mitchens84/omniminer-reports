## 📝 THESIS
While research has long centred on mothers, emerging science shows that becoming a father rewires men’s brains, lowers testosterone, and reshapes hormone profiles to favour caregiving. These biological shifts, combined with cultural changes in paternal involvement, produce measurable benefits for children, mothers, and fathers themselves.

## 💡 KEY INSIGHTS
1. **Brain remodeling mirrors motherhood** – Longitudinal MRI scans reveal gray‑matter loss in fathers 6‑12 months postpartum, streamlining networks for mentalising and social cognition, akin to changes observed in new mothers<sup>[1]</sup>.
2. **Testosterone dip supports a caregiving shift** – Fathers experience a natural decline in testosterone during pregnancy and early parenthood, reflecting a strategic move from mating competition to child investment<sup>[2]</sup>.
3. **Hormonal cues predict hands‑on care** – Elevated prolactin and oxytocin in dads are linked to more active childcare, underscoring a biologically driven motivation to nurture<sup>[3]</sup>.
4. **Paternal involvement is soaring** – Time‑use data (ATUS, American Time Use Survey) show dads now devote up to four times more daily childcare time than in the 1960s, a generational revolution<sup>[4]</sup>.
5. **Post‑partum depression affects fathers** – New dads report depressive symptoms at roughly twice the rate of the general population, driven by sleep loss, role stress, and neuro‑biological changes<sup>[5]</sup>.
6. **Children may keep dads’ brains younger** – UK Biobank analyses suggest parents of two‑three children have brain‑age estimates younger than child‑free peers, with diminishing returns after three kids<sup>[6]</sup>.
