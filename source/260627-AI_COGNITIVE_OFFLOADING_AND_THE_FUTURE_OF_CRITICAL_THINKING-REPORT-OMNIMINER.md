## ⚡ BOTTOM LINE
AI can erode critical thinking when used as a shortcut, but active, questioning‑based interaction can enhance learning; policy must steer use toward the latter.
---
## 📝 THESIS
The research landscape shows a nuanced picture: indiscriminate AI reliance dampens neural engagement and critical‑thinking performance, yet structured, Socratic engagement with AI can improve outcomes. This duality extends beyond students to all knowledge workers, demanding a calibrated response rather than blanket bans.
---
## 💡 KEY INSIGHTS
1. **Neural disengagement from AI‑assisted writing** – MIT’s pre‑print (2025) recorded the weakest brain‑wide connectivity among participants who used ChatGPT, with 83% unable to recall their own essays<sup>[1]</sup>.
2. **Confidence‑driven off‑loading** – The Carnegie Mellon/Microsoft CHI 2025 study of 390 professionals found that higher trust in AI correlated with reduced critical‑thinking effort, a pattern replicated in a 666‑person study showing a strong negative correlation between AI frequency and critical‑thinking scores<sup>[2]</sup>.
3. **Active engagement mitigates harm** – A 2024 PNAS paper demonstrated that students who interrogated, edited, and revised AI output performed better and reported less mental fatigue than passive copiers<sup>[3]</sup>.
4. **Device‑free interventions boost skills** – A Minneapolis teacher’s tech ban raised student confidence in reading from 46% to 95%; a Wharton study found similar grade gains for struggling college students when phones were removed<sup>[4]</sup>.
5. **Developmental stakes** – For children, AI can pre‑empt the formation of independent reasoning, risking permanent capacity loss, unlike adults who may merely experience temporary atrophy<sup>[5]</sup>.
