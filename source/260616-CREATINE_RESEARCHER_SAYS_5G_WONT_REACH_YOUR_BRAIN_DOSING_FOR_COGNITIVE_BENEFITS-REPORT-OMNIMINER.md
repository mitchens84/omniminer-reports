## 💡 KEY INSIGHTS

1. **The 5g ceiling is a muscle-centric dose** — Standard creatine dosing (5g/day) was established for muscle saturation. Dr. Candow contends this dose is largely sequestered by skeletal muscle and bone, leaving little to no surplus for brain uptake. The blood-brain barrier presents an additional hurdle, requiring higher circulating concentrations to drive meaningful transport into the central nervous system.<sup>[1]</sup>

2. **Cognitive benefits emerge under metabolic stress** — Creatine's primary role is as a rapid phosphate-donor for ATP regeneration. Under baseline conditions, the brain may already be creatine-replete, masking supplementation effects. However, under metabolic stress — sleep deprivation, high cognitive load, TBI recovery — demand exceeds supply, and supplemental creatine shows measurable cognitive improvements.<sup>[2]</sup>

3. **Alzheimer's pilot data is promising but early** — A 2025 University of Kansas pilot study reported an 11% increase in brain creatine levels and moderate cognitive improvements in Alzheimer's patients following creatine supplementation.<sup>[3]</sup> This is among the first human evidence that oral creatine can measurably alter brain creatine pools and cognitive function in neurodegeneration, but sample sizes are small and replication is needed.

4. **Creatine is a multi-tissue supplement** — Beyond muscle and brain, Candow's research highlights benefits for bone mineral density and mental health (depression, anxiety) across all ages and sexes. This broadens the case for creatine beyond sports nutrition into healthy ageing and geriatric medicine.<sup>[1]</sup>

## ⚔️ CONTRARIAN CORNER
**Steelman critique:** The body of evidence for brain creatine uptake from oral supplementation is thin. Most human studies show modest or null cognitive effects at standard doses, and the 2025 Alzheimer's pilot — while suggestive — is a small, single-site study. The 10g+ recommendation may outpace the evidence. Furthermore, higher creatine doses can cause digestive side effects and raise concerns about renal load in susceptible individuals.

**What would need to be true:** For the critique to be valid, we would need to see (a) negative results from adequately powered RCTs at 10g+ doses, (b) evidence that 5g dosing does achieve brain uptake when measured directly, or (c) safety signals that outweigh the modest upside of higher dosing.
