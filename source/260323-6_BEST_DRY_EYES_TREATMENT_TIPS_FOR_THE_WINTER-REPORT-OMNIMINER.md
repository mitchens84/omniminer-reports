## ⚡ BOTTOM LINE

Treat winter dry eyes by addressing four factors: reduce tear evaporation (humidifiers, avoiding airflow), maintain oil layer (lipid-based drops, warm compresses), protect from wind, and manage screen-time habits. The core mechanism is preserving tear film integrity in low-humidity environments.

---

## 🎯 OBJECTIVE

Provide actionable, evidence-based strategies to mitigate winter-exacerbated dry eye syndrome through environmental adjustments, topical treatments, and behavioural modifications.

---

## 📋 PREREQUISITES

- Access to artificial tear drops (preferably lipid-based)
- Humidifier (ideally with distilled water capacity)
- Ability to adjust indoor airflow (vents, fans)
- Sunglasses or wrap-around eyewear
- Clean cloth or commercial warm compress

---

## ⚙️ PROCESS

### Phase 1: Environmental Controls

1. **Redirect airflow** — Position heating vents, fans, and direct air sources away from face and eyes. In vehicles, direct vents downward or upward, not at face[^1].

2. **Increase ambient humidity** — Use a cool-mist humidifier with distilled water in frequently occupied rooms (bedroom, office). Target indoor humidity 40–60% to reduce tear evaporation[^1][✓].

### Phase 2: Tear Film Support

3. **Use lipid-based artificial tears** — Supplement natural tear film's oily layer with preservative-free artificial tears containing lipid/emollient components (e.g., Systane Balance, Refresh Optive Advanced). Apply 2–4 times daily[^1][✓].

4. **Perform daily warm compresses** — Apply warm (not hot) compress to closed eyelids for 5–10 minutes each morning to soften Meibomian gland secretions and improve lipid layer quality[^1][✓].

### Phase 3: Behavioural & Protective

5. **Implement the 20-20-20 rule** — Every 20 minutes of screen use, look ≥20 feet away for 20 seconds, with deliberate blinking to re-coat ocular surface. Screen viewing reduces blink rate by ~60%[^1][⚠].

6. **Wear protective eyewear** — Use wrap-around sunglasses outdoors to block wind; consider moisture-seal glasses (e.g., Ziena) indoors to shield from HVAC airflow[^1].

## 🔍 FACT CHECK

> ✓ **VERIFIED** — Cold winter air holds less moisture; indoor heating further lowers relative humidity, increasing tear evaporation. HVAC systems commonly reduce indoor humidity to 20–30% in winter, below the 40–60% range optimal for ocular comfort. Source: ASHRAE humidity standards and ophthalmology literature.[^1]

> ✓ **VERIFIED** — Lipid layer of tear film prevents evaporation; lipid-containing artificial tears can improve tear film stability in evaporative dry eye. Source: TFOS DEWS II Report (2017) on tear film composition.[^1]

> ⚠ **UNVERIFIED** — "We blink 60 less when we're looking at screens" — The phrasing is ambiguous; likely means "60% less." Studies show blink rate decreases by ~60–70% during digital screen use, though exact reduction varies. The claim is directionally correct but should be quantified as percentage reduction.

> ✓ **VERIFIED** — Warm compresses improve Meibomian gland function and are a first-line therapy for evaporative dry eye. Clinical guidelines (American Academy of Ophthalmology) recommend 5–10 minute compresses at 40–45°C.[^1]

## ⚔️ CONTRARIAN CORNER

**Steelman critique:** Winter indoor environments are not the primary driver of dry eye; rather, reduced outdoor time and lower vitamin D levels in winter may be more significant factors. Some patients find humidifiers increase dust mites and allergens, worsening ocular surface inflammation.

**What would need to be true:** Longitudinal studies would need to show no correlation between indoor humidity and dry eye symptoms when controlling for outdoor activity and vitamin D levels; humidifier use would need to demonstrably worsen allergic conjunctivitis in a significant subset.
