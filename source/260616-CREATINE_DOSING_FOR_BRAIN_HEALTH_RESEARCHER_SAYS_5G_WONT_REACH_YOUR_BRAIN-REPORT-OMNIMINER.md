## ⚡ BOTTOM LINE
Standard 5g creatine doses saturate muscle but not the brain. For cognitive benefits — especially under metabolic stress or in neurodegenerative conditions — 10g+ daily may be required, supported by emerging clinical evidence.

---

## 📝 THESIS
Dr. Darren Candow, a leading creatine researcher with 120+ publications, argues that creatine's well-established benefits for muscle, bone, and mental health do not translate to the brain at conventional doses. The blood-brain barrier limits creatine uptake, meaning cognitive effects require higher dosing strategies (10g+) and are most detectable when the brain is under metabolic stress, such as during sleep deprivation or neurodegenerative disease.

---

## 💡 KEY INSIGHTS

1. **The 5g ceiling is a muscle threshold, not a brain threshold** — Creatine is absorbed differently across tissues. Muscle tissue saturates readily at 5g/day, but the blood-brain barrier restricts transport, making the brain far harder to load. Dr. Candow explicitly states that 5g "won't reach your brain."<sup>[1]</sup> Doses of 10g or more may be necessary for measurable neurological effects.

2. **Cognitive benefits are conditional on metabolic stress** — Creatine's role as a rapid energy buffer (via ATP regeneration) becomes critical when brain energy demand exceeds supply. The most robust cognitive improvements from supplementation are observed under sleep deprivation, hypoxia, ageing, and neurodegeneration — not in healthy, rested individuals at baseline.<sup>[1]</sup>

3. **Alzheimer's pilot study shows promise** — A 2025 pilot study at the University of Kansas found that creatine supplementation produced an 11% increase in brain creatine levels alongside moderate cognitive improvements in Alzheimer's patients.<sup>[2]</sup> This provides early human evidence that the brain *can* be loaded with creatine and that it may confer cognitive benefit in pathological states.

4. **Creatine is a multi-tissue supplement** — Beyond muscle and brain, Candow's research programme (120+ papers) has demonstrated creatine's effects on bone density, mental health, and sarcopenia prevention across all ages and sexes.<sup>[3]</sup> This broadens the supplementation rationale beyond athletic performance into healthy ageing and longevity.
