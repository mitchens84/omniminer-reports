## 📝 THESIS
Popular culture has taken a mis‑interpreted 1940s zoo study of wolves and turned it into a meme of aggressive, hierarchical masculinity. Modern ethology shows that wild wolves are family units, chimpanzee rank is fluid and alliance‑based, and bonobo societies are female‑led and conflict‑averse. Human leadership should therefore be reframed around prestige, expertise and relational capital.

## 💡 KEY INSIGHTS
1. **Origin of the "alpha" myth** – Rudolf Schenkel’s 1947 captive‑wolf study coined “alpha animal”; the study’s artificial pack conditions produced aggressive dominance that does not exist in wild families<sup>[1]</sup>.
2. **Wolf packs are families** – Dave Mech’s long‑term field work demonstrated that the so‑called alpha pair are simply the breeding parents; offspring disperse rather than challenge them<sup>[2]</sup>.
3. **Pecking order’s true roots** – The term stems from Thorleif Schjelderup‑Ebbe’s 1920s chicken observations, not from wolves; it describes a dominance hierarchy that varies across species<sup>[3]</sup>.
4. **Chimpanzee dominance is relational** – High‑ranking males gain mating opportunities through alliances, grooming and reciprocal support, not constant aggression<sup>[4]</sup>.
5. **Bonobo societies model female‑led cooperation** – Conflict is often resolved through sexual behaviour, highlighting an alternative to male‑centric dominance<sup>[5]</sup>.
6. **Human power is prestige‑based** – Across cultures, knowledge, expertise and the ability to share information confer status more reliably than intimidation.
