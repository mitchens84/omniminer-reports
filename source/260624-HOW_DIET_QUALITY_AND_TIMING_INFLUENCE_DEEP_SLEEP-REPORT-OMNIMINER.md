## ⚡ BOTTOM LINE
Healthier dietary patterns—particularly Mediterranean and DASH—are consistently linked to longer, deeper sleep and lower insomnia risk, while high saturated‑fat, high‑sugar meals near bedtime impair sleep onset and deep‑sleep duration.

## 📝 THESIS
The relationship between nutrition and sleep is bidirectional, but emerging longitudinal evidence shows that diet quality can causally influence sleep outcomes. Both population‑level cohort analyses and controlled inpatient feeding studies converge on the finding that plant‑rich, low‑saturated‑fat diets improve sleep quantity and quality, especially when meals are spaced several hours before bedtime.

## 💡 KEY INSIGHTS
1. **Mediterranean diet predicts better sleep** — In the Multi‑Ethnic Study of Atherosclerosis (MESA), participants whose diets aligned with Mediterranean patterns had higher odds of adequate sleep duration and fewer insomnia symptoms<sup>[1]</sup>.
2. **DASH diet reduces insomnia risk** — A longitudinal analysis of the Women’s Health Initiative showed women adhering to DASH‑type diets were less likely to develop insomnia over a three‑year follow‑up<sup>[2]</sup>.
3. **Fiber boosts deep sleep; saturated fat and refined carbs blunt it** — In an inpatient sleep lab, higher fiber intake correlated with more slow‑wave sleep, while greater saturated‑fat consumption shortened deep sleep and increased arousals<sup>[3]</sup>.
4. **Self‑selected, higher‑fat meals delay sleep onset** — Participants who switched from a controlled, healthier diet to a self‑chosen, higher‑calorie, higher‑saturated‑fat diet took ~70 % longer to fall asleep and experienced ~20 % less deep sleep<sup>[3]</sup>.
5. **Meal timing matters** — Although not directly measured, the controlled feeding arm fixed dinner at 10 p.m. and yielded better sleep metrics than the free‑choice arm, supporting the recommendation to finish eating at least three hours before bed.
